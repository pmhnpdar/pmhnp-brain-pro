Smart Clinical Flags

☕
Caffeine
Assess in all anxiety/sleep complaints. ≥200mg/day worsens panic, insomnia, and ADHD-like symptoms.
💊
Benzos
Flag tolerance, dependence risk, cognitive impairment. Never abruptly stop. Taper 10%/2–4 weeks.
🧪
Substance Use
Impacts DA/mood stability. Assess frequency, quantity, functional impact at every visit.
🤰
Pregnancy
Risk vs. benefit discussion required. Untreated illness often riskier. Safer: sertraline, escitalopram. Coordinate with OB.
👴
Geriatric
Start at 50% adult dose. Avoid anticholinergics, benzos (Beers). Falls, cognition, renal clearance are key monitors.
❤️

QTc monitoring: ziprasidone, thioridazine, citalopram >40mg, IV haloperidol, TCAs. Baseline ECG when indicated.
⚖️
Metabolic
Weight, lipids, glucose with SGAs. Baseline + 3 months + annually. Olanzapine/clozapine highest risk.

Mood Disorders

MDD · Bipolar · Dysthymia · Cyclothymia

🌧 Mood Disorders — Diagnostic Framework
Presenting Patterns
  • Depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day (subjective or observed)
  • Markedly diminished interest or pleasure in almost all activities (anhedonia)
  • Fatigue or loss of energy, psychomotor changes (agitation or retardation)
  • Sleep disturbance (hypersomnia or insomnia), appetite/weight changes
  • Cognitive slowing, concentration impairment, indecisiveness
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive/inappropriate guilt
  • Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation
Primary DSM-5-TR Diagnoses
  • MDD — ≥5 SIGECAPS symptoms for ≥2 weeks, at least one = depressed mood or anhedonia
  • PDD (Dysthymia) — Depressed mood most days for ≥2 years (≥1 year in children), ≥2 symptoms
  • Bipolar I — ≥1 manic episode (≥7 days or hospitalization-level); depressive episodes typical
  • Bipolar II — ≥1 hypomanic episode (≥4 days, no hospitalization) + major depressive episodes
  • Cyclothymia — ≥2 years hypomanic + depressive symptoms never meeting full criteria
  • Substance/Medication-Induced MDD — Rule out first
  • MDD with Psychotic Features — Mood-congruent delusions/hallucinations during episode
Differentials & Rule-outs
ConsiderLess Likely If
Bipolar — administer MDQ; any hx of hypomanic/manic episode?No energy surges, no decreased need for sleep, no grandiosity ever
Adjustment Disorder — clear identifiable stressor <6 months agoSymptoms predate the stressor; full SIGECAPS criteria met
Grief — normal bereavementProminent SI, psychosis, or severe functional impairment beyond grief
Hypothyroidism — TSH ordered?Normal thyroid function confirmed on labs
Sleep Apnea — fatigue, hypersomnia, snoringFatigue resolves with sleep, no partner-reported apneas
Medical Mimics & Substances
  • Hypothyroidism / hyperthyroidism (TSH)
  • Anemia (CBC, ferritin) — fatigue, cognitive fog
  • Vitamin D deficiency (<30 ng/mL) — seasonal, fatigue, low mood
  • B12 / folate deficiency — depression, cognitive symptoms
  • Testosterone deficiency (males) — low energy, anhedonia, low libido
  • Chronic pain syndromes — bidirectional relationship with depression
  • OSA — fatigue, cognitive impairment, depressed mood
  • Medications: beta-blockers, corticosteroids, isotretinoin, hormonal contraceptives
  • Substances: alcohol (depressant — assess use), cannabis (amotivational)
🚨 Red Flags — Higher Level of Care
  • Active SI with plan, intent, or means — safety planning, consider hospitalization
  • Psychotic features — delusions, hallucinations during mood episode
  • Severe functional impairment — unable to care for self, children, or perform basic ADLs
  • Active manic episode — requires mood stabilizer or antipsychotic; avoid antidepressant monotherapy
  • Postpartum psychosis — psychiatric emergency, requires inpatient evaluation
  • Catatonia — stupor, waxy flexibility, mutism — rule out NMS, treat with lorazepam/ECT
Suggested Scales
  • PHQ-9 — severity of depression (administer at every visit; monitor item 9 for SI)
  • MDQ — Mood Disorder Questionnaire — screen for bipolar spectrum if any hypomanic features
  • PHQ-2 — ultra-brief 2-item screen for initial triage
  • GAD-7 — frequently comorbid; administer alongside PHQ-9

Anxiety Disorders

GAD · Panic · Social · Specific Phobia · Agoraphobia

😰 Anxiety Disorders — Diagnostic Framework
Presenting Patterns
  • Excessive, persistent worry across multiple domains (GAD)
  • Sudden onset of intense fear with somatic symptoms — panic attacks
  • Fear of social situations, scrutiny, embarrassment (Social Anxiety)
  • Intrusive, unwanted thoughts with compulsive behaviors (OCD)
  • Avoidance of anxiety-provoking situations or stimuli
  • Muscle tension, restlessness, fatigue, irritability, sleep disruption
Primary DSM-5-TR Diagnoses
  • GAD — excessive worry ≥6 months, ≥3 symptoms (restlessness, fatigue, concentration, irritability, muscle tension, sleep)
  • Panic Disorder — recurrent unexpected panic attacks + persistent concern/behavioral change ≥1 month
  • Social Anxiety Disorder — marked fear of social situations, fear of humiliation, avoidance
  • Specific Phobia — marked, excessive fear of specific object/situation
  • Agoraphobia — fear of ≥2 situations (public transport, open spaces, crowds, alone outside)
  • OCD — obsessions + compulsions ≥1h/day; ego-dystonic; significant impairment (note: now separate chapter in DSM-5-TR)
  • Separation Anxiety — excessive fear of separation from attachment figures
Differentials & Rule-outs
ConsiderLess Likely If
Cardiac arrhythmia — palpitations, syncopeNormal ECG/Holter; symptoms purely situational
Hyperthyroidism — anxiety, palpitations, tremor, heat intoleranceNormal TSH
Pheochromocytoma — episodic hypertension, headache, sweating, palpitationsNo episodic hypertension; urine catecholamines normal
Substance-induced — caffeine, stimulants, cannabis (acute), withdrawal (alcohol, benzos)Symptoms persist after substance cessation
PTSD — anxiety + identifiable trauma history + hypervigilance, avoidanceNo trauma history; no re-experiencing symptoms
ADHD — anxiety-driven inattention vs. ADHD inattentionInattention present in all contexts, not just worry-driven
🚨 Red Flags
  • Chest pain, syncope, or palpitations — cardiac rule-out required before anxiolytic treatment
  • Panic attacks with new-onset presentation in older adult — cardiac/neurological workup
  • OCD with dangerous compulsions (self-harm, contamination leading to malnutrition)
  • Agoraphobia so severe patient is housebound — PHP/IOP level of care
  • Severe social anxiety in adolescent impeding school attendance
Suggested Scales
  • GAD-7 — severity of generalized anxiety (≥10 = moderate)
  • PCL-5 — if trauma history (≥33 = probable PTSD)
  • Y-BOCS — OCD severity (0–40; ≥16 = moderate-severe)
  • PHQ-9 — frequent comorbidity

Psychotic Disorders

Schizophrenia · Schizoaffective · Brief Psychotic · Delusional

🔮 Psychosis — Diagnostic Framework
Presenting Patterns
  • Auditory or visual hallucinations (command, persecutory, referential)
  • Fixed false beliefs (delusions) — persecutory, grandiose, erotomanic, somatic
  • Disorganized speech (loose associations, tangentiality, word salad)
  • Grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior
  • Negative symptoms — flat affect, alogia, avolition, anhedonia, asociality
  • Declining function in work, school, self-care, or relationships
Primary DSM-5-TR Diagnoses
  • Schizophrenia — ≥2 Criterion A symptoms ≥1 month; continuous disturbance ≥6 months; functional decline
  • Schizoaffective — schizophrenia criteria + major mood episode; ≥2 weeks psychosis without mood symptoms
  • Brief Psychotic Disorder — ≥1 Criterion A symptom, 1 day to <1 month; return to premorbid function
  • Schizophreniform — schizophrenia criteria, 1–6 months
  • Delusional Disorder — non-bizarre delusions ≥1 month; no Criterion A otherwise
  • Bipolar I with Psychotic Features — psychosis during manic or depressive episode
  • MDD with Psychotic Features — mood-congruent hallucinations/delusions during MDE
Differentials & Rule-outs
ConsiderLess Likely If
Substance-induced psychosis — cannabis, stimulants, PCP, LSD, alcohol withdrawalSymptoms persist >1 month after substance cessation
Delirium — acute onset, fluctuating consciousness, medical etiologyClear consciousness, no acute medical illness
Autoimmune encephalitis — anti-NMDA receptor (young women, acute psychiatric + neurological sx)No movement disorder, no autonomic instability, no seizures
DLB (Dementia with Lewy Bodies) — visual hallucinations + parkinsonism + fluctuating cognition in elderlyNo parkinsonism; no cognitive fluctuation
Temporal lobe epilepsy — olfactory hallucinations, episodic symptoms, postictal statesNo stereotyped episodes; normal EEG
🚨 Red Flags
  • Command hallucinations directing violence toward self or others
  • First-episode psychosis — inpatient evaluation often required; LAI consideration
  • Agitation with risk of harm to self or others
  • Inability to care for self — unable to eat, maintain hygiene, attend to medical needs
  • DLB suspected — avoid antipsychotics (can cause fatal neuroleptic sensitivity)
  • Catatonia — medical emergency; do not mistake for negative symptoms
Suggested Scales
  • PANSS (Positive and Negative Syndrome Scale) — clinical monitoring
  • BPRS (Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale) — brief inpatient monitoring
  • AIMS — tardive dyskinesia monitoring (baseline before antipsychotics, then q6mo)
  • PHQ-9 — comorbid depression (very common in schizophrenia)

Substance Use Disorders

AUD · OUD · Stimulant · Cannabis · Tobacco · Polysubstance

🧪 Substance & Behavioral — Diagnostic Framework
Key SUD Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR)

≥2 of 11 criteria within 12 months = Substance Use Disorder. Severity: Mild (2–3), Moderate (4–5), Severe (≥6).

  • Using more or longer than intended
  • Persistent desire or unsuccessful efforts to cut down
  • Significant time spent obtaining, using, or recovering
  • Craving or strong urge to use
  • Failure to fulfill role obligations
  • Continued use despite social/interpersonal problems
  • Giving up important activities
  • Use in hazardous situations
  • Continued use despite physical/psychological problems
  • Tolerance — diminished effect with same amount
  • Withdrawal — characteristic syndrome on cessation
🚨 Red Flags — Medical Emergencies
  • Alcohol withdrawal — seizure risk (especially >7 drinks/day chronic); CIWA-Ar protocol; thiamine BEFORE glucose
  • Opioid withdrawal — severe physical distress; buprenorphine or methadone initiation
  • Overdose risk — prescribe naloxone (Narcan) to all patients with OUD or high-dose opioid use
  • Benzodiazepine withdrawal — seizure risk; NEVER abruptly discontinue long-term use
  • Stimulant-induced psychosis — usually resolves 1–2 weeks off substance
Suggested Scales
  • AUDIT — Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test (≥8 = hazardous use)
  • CAGE-AID — rapid 4-item screen for alcohol/drug use (≥2 = positive)
  • DAST-10 — Drug Abuse Screening Test
  • CIWA-Ar — Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment (use for alcohol detox monitoring)

Trauma & Stressor-Related

PTSD · Acute Stress · Adjustment Disorder · Complex Trauma

🛡 Trauma & PTSD — Diagnostic Framework
PTSD DSM-5-TR Criteria Overview
  • Criterion A — Exposure to actual/threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence
  • Criterion B — Intrusion symptoms (flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive memories, distress)
  • Criterion C — Avoidance (thoughts/feelings, external reminders)
  • Criterion D — Negative alterations in cognition/mood (distorted blame, negative emotional state, anhedonia, detachment)
  • Criterion E — Hyperarousal (hypervigilance, exaggerated startle, sleep disturbance, reckless behavior, irritability/anger)
  • Duration — >1 month; significant distress or functional impairment
Key Differentials
DifferentialDistinguishing Feature
PTSD vs. Bipolar IIBipolar: mood episodes last days-weeks; PTSD: mood tied to trauma triggers
PTSD vs. BPDBPD: identity disturbance, splitting; frequent co-occurrence — assess both
PTSD vs. MDDPTSD: re-experiencing required; MDD: pervasive without identifiable trauma trigger
PTSD vs. Adjustment DisorderAD: stressor may not be traumatic; resolves within 6 months of stressor end
Suggested Scales
  • PCL-5 — 20-item PTSD checklist (≥33 = probable PTSD; monitor treatment response)
  • PHQ-9 — comorbid depression (very common with PTSD)
  • GAD-7 — comorbid anxiety

Neurodevelopmental

ADHD · ASD · Specific Learning Disorder · Tic Disorders

⚡ Attention & Executive — Diagnostic Framework
ADHD Diagnostic Criteria (DSM-5-TR)

≥6 inattentive OR hyperactive/impulsive symptoms (≥5 if ≥17 years old); present in ≥2 settings; onset before age 12; not better explained by another disorder.

Inattention cluster: careless mistakes, sustained attention, doesn't seem to listen, fails to follow through, organizational difficulty, avoids mental effort, loses things, easily distracted, forgetful.

Hyperactive/impulsive cluster: fidgets, leaves seat, runs/climbs inappropriately (or restless in adults), unable to engage quietly, "on the go," talks excessively, blurts out, difficulty waiting turn, interrupts.

Key Differentials
DifferentialDistinguishing Feature
ADHD vs. AnxietyAnxiety: inattention driven by worry; ADHD: inattention present even without worry, pervasive across contexts
ADHD vs. BipolarBipolar: episodic hypomanic energy; ADHD: chronic, non-episodic, onset in childhood
ADHD vs. DepressionDepression: cognitive slowing, onset with mood episode; ADHD: lifelong pattern
ADHD vs. ASDASD: social communication deficits, restricted interests; can co-occur with ADHD
ADHD vs. PTSDPTSD: concentration difficulty tied to hypervigilance/trauma triggers; ACE history key
Suggested Scales
  • ASRS-v1.1 — Adult ADHD Self-Report Scale (6-item screener; ≥4 shaded items = positive)
  • Conners Adult ADHD Rating Scale — comprehensive assessment
  • PHQ-9 + GAD-7 — assess comorbid mood/anxiety first

Sleep-Wake Disorders

Insomnia · Hypersomnia · Narcolepsy · RLS · Parasomnias

🌙 Sleep & Circadian — Diagnostic Framework
Primary Sleep Disorders in Psychiatric Context
  • Insomnia Disorder — difficulty initiating/maintaining sleep ≥3 nights/week for ≥3 months; daytime impairment; most often comorbid with psychiatric disorders
  • OSA — excessive daytime sleepiness, snoring, apneas; refer for polysomnography; CPAP first-line
  • Restless Legs Syndrome — urge to move legs, worse at rest/night; assess iron levels (ferritin <75 ng/mL → supplement); dopamine agonists
  • REM Sleep Behavior Disorder — acting out dreams, violent movements; associated with Parkinson's/DLB — neurological referral
  • Circadian Rhythm Disorder — delayed sleep phase (adolescents) or advanced sleep phase (elderly); light therapy + melatonin
Psychiatry-Sleep Bidirectional Relationship
  • Insomnia is both a symptom AND a risk factor for depression/anxiety — treat it explicitly
  • Hypersomnia: bipolar depression, MDD with atypical features, medication side effects
  • Early morning awakening: classic melancholic depression — highly sensitive indicator of HPA axis dysregulation
  • Sleep deprivation can precipitate manic episodes in bipolar — sleep is a treatment target
  • PTSD nightmares: specific treatment target (prazosin, IRT therapy)

Personality Disorders

BPD · NPD · OCPD · Antisocial · Cluster A/B/C

🪞 Personality — Diagnostic Framework
BPD vs. Bipolar — Critical Distinction
FeatureBPD Mood ShiftsBipolar II Mood Episodes
DurationMinutes to hoursDays to weeks
TriggerInterpersonal events (rejection, abandonment)Often spontaneous or circadian
Sleep needVariable, not decreasedDecreased need (not just insomnia) in hypomania
IdentityUnstable sense of self, splittingIntact identity outside episodes
Co-occurrenceFrequent — assess both; not mutually exclusiveBPD + Bipolar II is common
Treatment Approach for Personality Disorders
  • BPD — DBT is gold standard; no FDA-approved medications for BPD itself; treat comorbidities (mood, anxiety, psychosis) pharmacologically; mood stabilizers for affective instability; low-dose antipsychotics for brief psychotic episodes
  • Avoid: benzodiazepines in BPD (disinhibition, dependence risk); multiple medication changes in response to emotional crises
  • OCPD — SSRIs for rigidity/anxiety; CBT addressing perfectionism and control
  • ASPD — address comorbid SUD; behavioral approaches; safety management
Treatment Pathways

Treat

Stepwise, evidence-based algorithms by disorder. Start low, go slow. Ensure adequate trial (4–8 weeks) before escalating.

Adequate trial = therapeutic dose × 4–6 weeks (8 weeks for full response assessment). Document dose, duration, and response for every prior trial.
1
First-Line

SSRI or SNRI — First-Line Antidepressant

  • Sertraline 25–50mg → 100–200mg | Best overall tolerability, fewest drug interactions
  • Escitalopram 10mg → 20mg | Cleanest CYP profile, excellent tolerability
  • Duloxetine 30mg → 60–120mg | Add if pain comorbidity, stress urinary incontinence
  • Venlafaxine XR 37.5mg → 75–225mg | Stronger NE at higher doses; BP monitoring
  • Bupropion XL 150mg → 300mg | Best if fatigue/anhedonia/weight/sexual SE concern; avoid seizure hx
  • Address lifestyle: sleep hygiene, caffeine, exercise (150 min/week moderate aerobic = antidepressant equivalent)
  • Add therapy referral: CBT, behavioral activation, or supportive therapy
2
Optimize

Maximize Before Switching

  • Ensure maximum tolerated dose before declaring failure
  • Confirm adherence — prescription refill patterns, pharmacy records
  • Extend trial to 8 full weeks if partial response at 4–6 weeks
  • Activate CBT — combined treatment superior to monotherapy
  • Address sleep, caffeine, alcohol intake, and exercise concurrently
  • Re-screen: Is this actually bipolar depression? (MDQ, longitudinal hx)
3
Switch

Cross-Taper to Different Agent or Class

  • Inadequate response after adequate trial → switch, don't layer
  • SSRI → SNRI or vice versa (same class switch may work if partial response)
  • Consider mirtazapine if insomnia + poor appetite are prominent (sedating, weight gain)
  • Consider bupropion if fatigue, anhedonia, sexual SE, or weight gain from prior SSRI
  • Consider vortioxetine (Trintellix) if cognitive symptoms prominent — unique multimodal mechanism
  • Consider vilazodone (Viibryd) if GI tolerability was key concern — take with food
  • Document prior trial before switching: drug, dose, duration, reason for d/c
4
Augment

Augmentation Strategies

  • Atypical antipsychotics (FDA-approved augmentation): Aripiprazole 2–5mg, Quetiapine XR 50–150mg, Brexpiprazole 1–3mg — monitor metabolic
  • Lithium 300mg BID → target level 0.6–0.8 mEq/L — classic augmentator; monitor renal/thyroid
  • Thyroid (T3) — Liothyronine 25–50mcg QAM — especially if low-normal thyroid function
  • Bupropion + SSRI — addresses residual energy, libido; monitor for serotonin syndrome at high doses
  • L-methylfolate 15mg — especially if MTHFR polymorphism; add to existing antidepressant
  • Omega-3 (EPA >1g/day) — anti-inflammatory; add to existing antidepressant
  • Mirtazapine + SSRI/SNRI (California Rocket Fuel) — complementary mechanisms; sedation + weight gain tradeoff
5
Refractory / Specialist

TRD — Advanced Interventions

  • Esketamine (Spravato) — intranasal, 56 or 84mg 2x/week × 4 weeks, then weekly. REMS program. Observe 2h post-dose. Onset within hours.
  • IV Ketamine — 0.5mg/kg over 40 min × 6 infusions. Off-label, infusion clinics.
  • Auvelity (DXM/Bupropion) — 45/105mg × 3 days then BID. Novel NMDA mechanism. GI/dizziness common initially.
  • ECT — Most effective treatment for TRD, psychotic depression, catatonia, severe SI. 3×/week × 6–12 sessions. Anesthesia required.
  • rTMS — Outpatient, no anesthesia, 5 days/week × 4–6 weeks. Less effective than ECT. Good for patients unable/unwilling to consider ECT.
  • Specialist referral to TRD program if above unavailable
1
First-Line

SSRI or SNRI + Psychoeducation

  • Escitalopram 10mg → 20mg | Sertraline 25mg → 100–200mg | Paroxetine 10mg → 40mg
  • Venlafaxine XR 37.5mg → 75–225mg | Duloxetine 30mg → 60–120mg
  • Buspirone 7.5mg BID → 15–30mg BID (onset 2–4 weeks; non-sedating, non-habit forming, no withdrawal)
  • No benzodiazepines as first-line — reserve for acute bridging only
  • Counsel: reduce caffeine, assess alcohol (worsens anxiety rebound), improve sleep consistency
  • Refer for CBT — worry/rumination protocol
2
Optimize

Optimize + Adjunct PRN

  • Ensure therapeutic dose — anxiety patients often need full antidepressant dose range
  • Allow 4–6 weeks at target dose before assessing response
  • Hydroxyzine 25–50mg PRN — rapid anxiolytic, non-addictive; useful for breakthrough anxiety
  • CBT engagement — worry log, scheduling worry time, interoceptive exposure for somatic anxiety
  • Sleep is a target: address sleep to reduce anxiety burden
3
Switch

Cross-Taper to Alternative

  • Switch SSRI → SNRI or vice versa
  • Add buspirone if not already trialed
  • Consider mirtazapine if sleep disruption prominent (sedating, anti-anxiety via H1 + alpha-2 blockade)
4
Augment

Augmentation Options

  • Pregabalin 75–300mg BID — off-label, controlled substance (check state); effective for GAD; avoid abrupt d/c
  • Quetiapine XR 50–150mg — evidence for GAD, sedating; reserve for refractory or comorbid depression
  • Magnesium glycinate 200–400mg QHS — reduces neuronal hyperexcitability; adjunctive
  • Ashwagandha KSM-66 300mg BID — cortisol reduction, moderate evidence for stress/anxiety
5
Refractory

Refractory GAD

  • Time-limited benzodiazepine (lorazepam 0.5–1mg BID) with clear taper plan — reassess at 4–6 weeks
  • Low-dose antipsychotic (quetiapine, trifluoperazine — evidence-based for GAD)
  • Intensive outpatient CBT / specialized anxiety program
  • Rule out undiagnosed bipolar, PTSD, ADHD as drivers of anxiety
⚠️ Critical Rule: Do NOT initiate antidepressant monotherapy in bipolar depression without mood stabilizer coverage. Risk of triggering mania, rapid cycling, or mixed states. MDQ screen before ANY antidepressant.
1
First-Line (Mood Stabilizer Based)

Mood Stabilizer or Atypical Antipsychotic FIRST

  • Quetiapine (Seroquel) 50mg QHS → 300mg — FDA-approved bipolar depression; sedation + metabolic risk
  • Lurasidone (Latuda) 20mg → 60–120mg QD with food — FDA-approved BP depression; weight-neutral, low metabolic risk
  • Lithium 300mg BID → target level 0.8–1.0 mEq/L — gold standard mood stabilizer; monitor renal, thyroid, Ca, ECG
  • Lamotrigine (Lamictal) 25mg → 100–200mg — effective for bipolar depression; SLOW titration mandatory (6–8 weeks) to prevent SJS
  • Cariprazine (Vraylar) 1.5mg → 3mg — FDA-approved bipolar depression and mania; activating, low weight gain
2
Optimize

Stabilize Before Adding

  • Optimize sleep — sleep disruption can trigger episodes; melatonin, consistent wake time
  • Assess for rapid cycling (>4 episodes/year) — avoid antidepressants; valproate preferred
  • Assess mixed features — antidepressants contraindicated in mixed episodes
  • Substance use assessment — cannabis and alcohol destabilize bipolar significantly
  • Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT) — psychotherapy of choice for bipolar
3
Add Antidepressant (With Caution)

SSRI Adjunctive — Only After Mood Stabilizer Established

  • If MDE persists despite adequate mood stabilizer: add SSRI (bupropion or sertraline preferred — lower switch risk)
  • Avoid paroxetine (highest switch risk), TCAs, SNRIs at high doses — greater risk of mania induction
  • Plan to taper antidepressant once remission achieved — don't maintain long-term in bipolar unless necessary
  • Monitor weekly × 4 weeks for emergence of hypomanic/manic symptoms after adding antidepressant
4
Augment

Combination Strategies

  • Lithium + lamotrigine — complementary mechanisms; adjust lamotrigine dose (lithium has minimal interaction but monitor)
  • Lithium + lurasidone — FDA-approved combination for BP depression
  • Thyroid T3 augmentation — especially if low-normal thyroid on labs
  • NAC 1200–2400mg/day — glutamate modulator, evidence in bipolar depression
  • Omega-3 EPA >1g/day — anti-inflammatory augmentation
5
Refractory

Refractory Bipolar Depression

  • ECT — most effective for severe/psychotic bipolar depression; prevents suicide; mood stabilizer should continue
  • Ketamine/Esketamine — use with extreme caution in bipolar; may trigger switch to mania; ensure mood stabilizer coverage
  • Specialist/academic center referral
  • MAOI (tranylcypromine) — specialist use only; dietary restrictions required
1
First-Line Stimulant

Stimulant Monotherapy

  • Amphetamine class (mixed salts XR, lisdexamfetamine): 10–20mg → 20–60mg; more potent, longer duration
  • Methylphenidate class (Concerta, Ritalin LA, Focalin): 18–36mg → 54–72mg; different mechanism, may be preferred
  • Titrate every 1–2 weeks based on response and tolerability
  • Ensure coverage matches impairment windows: work/school + late afternoon if needed
  • Cardiac screening: BP/HR before and after each dose increase; ECG if family history of sudden cardiac death
2
Optimize Coverage

Full-Day Coverage Strategy

  • Add afternoon IR booster if XR wears off before end of work/school
  • Address appetite suppression: eat breakfast before dose; high-protein dinner
  • Sleep: stimulants can delay sleep onset; melatonin 0.5–1mg or clonidine 0.1mg QHS
  • Address emotional dysregulation (RSD) — often the most impairing feature in adults
  • Drug holidays on weekends/summers: discuss benefit vs. social/family impact
3
Switch Stimulant Class

If One Class Fails, Try the Other

  • Methylphenidate non-response: try amphetamine class (different mechanism — NE/DA reuptake vs. release)
  • Amphetamine non-response or SE: try methylphenidate class
  • Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse): if abuse concern; prodrug pharmacology limits misuse potential
4
Non-Stimulant

Non-Stimulant Alternatives

  • Atomoxetine (Strattera) 40mg → 80–100mg QAM — NE selective; 6–8 week full trial; anxiety comorbidity; SUD patients; watch hepatotoxicity and SI in children
  • Viloxazine (Qelbree) 100–200mg → 400mg — newer NE reuptake inhibitor; fewer SE than atomoxetine; once-daily
  • Bupropion 150–300mg — off-label; NE/DA; useful when depression comorbid; no SE on appetite/sleep; avoid seizure hx
  • Guanfacine ER (Intuniv) 1–4mg QHS — adjunct for hyperactivity/impulsivity residual; first-line in kids if stimulant not appropriate; sedation, hypotension
  • Clonidine ER (Kapvay) — similar to guanfacine; also targets sleep, tics in comorbid presentations
5
Refractory / Complex

Complex ADHD

  • Neuropsychological testing if diagnosis uncertain or learning disability suspected
  • Address comorbid anxiety first — uncontrolled anxiety may be driving inattention
  • Stimulant + guanfacine/clonidine combination for residual hyperactivity
  • Stimulant + bupropion for motivation/anhedonia + ADHD
  • Consider EEG biofeedback (neurofeedback) — emerging evidence
1
First-Line: Behavioral

CBT-I (Gold Standard)

  • CBT-I is first-line over medications per AASM 2023 guidelines
  • Sleep restriction therapy + stimulus control + sleep hygiene + cognitive restructuring

Address Comorbidities

Treat Underlying Drivers

  • Pain → duloxetine, gabapentin; refer for pain management

Avoid / Cautions

What to Deprescribe

  • Avoid long-term benzodiazepines — dependence, tolerance, cognitive impairment, fall risk (elderly)

Before Escalating — Checklist

  • Document ≥2 failed adequate trials in current episode

Rapid-Acting Glutamatergic Interventions

ECT / rTMS

Neurostimulation

  • ECT — Most effective treatment for TRD (60–80% response). Also for psychotic depression, catatonia, severe SI, high lethality risk. 3×/week × 6–12 sessions. General anesthesia. Cognitive SE (retrograde amnesia, usually transient). Maintenance ECT for high relapse risk.

CYP450 Drug Interactions

Cytochrome P450 substrates, inhibitors, and inducers — psychiatric focus

For licensed healthcare providers only. Educational use only. Not medical advice.

CYP450 Quick Reference

The CYP450 system metabolizes ~75% of psychiatric medications. Know the key enzymes — especially 1A2, 2C19, 2D6, 3A4 — to prevent dangerous interactions and optimize dosing.

SUBSTRATE = metabolized by enzyme INHIBITOR = slows enzyme → ↑ other drug levels INDUCER = speeds enzyme → ↓ other drug levels

CYP 1A2

Inducible by smoking; inhibited by fluvoxamine

SubstratesClozapine, Olanzapine, Haloperidol, Fluvoxamine, Mirtazapine, Duloxetine, Caffeine, Theophylline
InhibitorsFluvoxamine (strong), Ciprofloxacin, Cimetidine
InducersSmoking (tobacco), Carbamazepine, Rifampin, Omeprazole (weak)
Pearl: Smokers need higher clozapine/olanzapine doses. If patient quits smoking → levels rise → monitor for toxicity.

CYP 2C19

Genetic polymorphism — PM, IM, EM, UM

SubstratesCitalopram, Escitalopram, Sertraline, Diazepam, Phenytoin, Omeprazole, Clopidogrel (prodrug)
InhibitorsFluoxetine (mod), Fluvoxamine (strong), Omeprazole, Esomeprazole
InducersCarbamazepine, Rifampin, St. John's Wort
Pearl: Poor metabolizers (PMs) have higher citalopram/escitalopram levels → QTc risk. Ultra-rapid metabolizers may need higher doses.

CYP 2D6

Most polymorphic; ~8% Caucasians are PMs

SubstratesFluoxetine, Paroxetine, Venlafaxine, TCAs (most), Haloperidol, Risperidone, Aripiprazole, Atomoxetine, Codeine, Tramadol, Metoprolol
InhibitorsFluoxetine (strong), Paroxetine (strong), Bupropion (strong), Duloxetine (mod), Sertraline (mild)
InducersCarbamazepine (weak), Rifampin
Pearl: Fluoxetine/paroxetine + TCA = TCA toxicity risk. Bupropion + aripiprazole → ↑ aripiprazole levels → akathisia risk. PMs on tramadol → ↓ analgesia (need active metabolite).

CYP 3A4

Most abundant hepatic enzyme; metabolizes ~50% of all drugs

SubstratesAlprazolam, Triazolam, Midazolam, Clonazepam, Buspirone, Quetiapine, Lurasidone, Cariprazine, Ziprasidone, Carbamazepine, Lamotrigine (partial)
InhibitorsFluvoxamine (strong), Nefazodone (strong), Grapefruit juice, Ketoconazole, Erythromycin, Clarithromycin
InducersCarbamazepine (strong), St. John's Wort, Rifampin, Phenytoin, Oxcarbazepine
Pearl: Carbamazepine induces its own metabolism (autoinduction over 2–4 weeks). Lurasidone/quetiapine + 3A4 inhibitor → toxicity. Remind patients: no grapefruit with benzodiazepines.

Child & Adolescent Psychiatry

Ages 6–17 | FDA-approved indications differ significantly from adults | Start low, titrate slowly | Involve family in all decisions

Black Box Warning

All antidepressants carry a black box warning for increased suicidal ideation in patients <25 years old. This does NOT mean avoid — untreated depression is riskier. Monitor weekly for the first 4 weeks after initiation or dose change.

FDA-Approved Antidepressants (Peds)

  • Always rule out bipolar before starting antidepressant in adolescents

  • Prioritize therapy (CBT) before or alongside meds when feasible
  • Obtain baseline metabolic panel before antipsychotics; recheck at 3 months

  • Screen for trauma, abuse, and substance use at every visit

Women's Mental Health & Perinatal Psychiatry

Pregnancy, Postpartum, Perimenopause | Risk/benefit framing essential | Untreated illness is ALSO a risk

General Principle

Untreated psychiatric illness in pregnancy carries significant risks: preterm birth, low birth weight, poor prenatal care, substance use, suicide. Risk/benefit discussion must always be documented. Coordinate with OB/GYN.

Antidepressants in Pregnancy

  • Sertraline: Most safety data; preferred first-line; no major structural defects
  • Escitalopram/Citalopram: Reasonable; minor QTc risk in neonate at high doses
  • Fluoxetine: Good safety data; long half-life advantageous if adherence concern
  • Paroxetine: Avoid — cardiac defects (VSD) in first trimester, neonatal withdrawal
  • Bupropion: Limited data; possible cardiac risk in first trimester; avoid if possible
  • Venlafaxine: Use if clinically needed; neonatal adaptation syndrome risk

Meds to AVOID in Pregnancy

  • Valproate: Neural tube defects, cognitive teratogenicity — CONTRAINDICATED in women of reproductive age unless fully informed, on contraception
  • Lithium (T1): Ebstein anomaly risk (small but real); if needed, switch to alternate or closely monitor fetal echo
  • Benzodiazepines (chronic): Neonatal withdrawal; avoid long-term; occasional use less risky
  • Carbamazepine: Neural tube defects; avoid
  • Clonazepam: If needed: use lowest effective dose; taper prior to delivery

Postpartum Depression (PPD)

  • Screen with Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) at 4–6 weeks postpartum and at 6 months
  • Mild-moderate PPD: Therapy (IPT, CBT) ± SSRI (sertraline preferred — lowest breast milk transfer)
  • Severe PPD / PPD with psychosis: SSRI + antipsychotic; consider hospitalization
  • Brexanolone (Zulresso): IV neuroactive steroid; 60h infusion; REMS; fastest onset for severe PPD
  • Zuranolone (Zurzuvae): Oral; 14-day course; FDA approved 2023; GABA-A PAM
  • Breastfeeding preferred agents: Sertraline, Paroxetine (low breast milk), Nortriptyline

Perimenopause & Menopause

  • Estrogen fluctuation → mood instability, anxiety, cognitive fog, insomnia
  • Rule out thyroid dysfunction (TSH, free T4) before psychiatric label
  • Antidepressants: SSRIs/SNRIs effective for hot flashes + mood; desvenlafaxine, venlafaxine preferred
  • HRT: Coordinate with GYN; estrogen can enhance antidepressant response
  • Gabapentin: Hot flashes + sleep + anxiety; 100–300mg TID
  • Psychotherapy + lifestyle crucial; validate experience

Clinical Pearls

  • Document risk/benefit discussion clearly; patient must verbalize understanding
  • Neonatal Adaptation Syndrome: jitteriness, tremor, feeding difficulties; resolves in 2 weeks; NOT a reason to avoid SSRIs
  • Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension (PPHN): rare (~1:500) with late-pregnancy SSRI; risk does NOT outweigh benefit of treating severe illness
  • PMDD: SSRIs (intermittent luteal-phase dosing works); track symptoms with diary

Geriatric Psychiatry

Ages 65+ | Start low, go slow | Beers Criteria | Renal/hepatic adjustments | Falls risk always

Beers Criteria — Avoid

  • Benzodiazepines: All — falls, cognitive impairment, paradoxical agitation
  • Diphenhydramine (Benadryl): Strong anticholinergic → delirium, cognitive decline
  • TCAs (especially amitriptyline, imipramine): Anticholinergic, orthostasis, QTc
  • First-gen antipsychotics: EPS, QTc, falls; use cautiously even if needed
  • Zolpidem/Z-drugs: Falls, cognitive impairment; use melatonin, low-dose doxepin instead
  • Anticholinergics in dementia: Urinary meds (oxybutynin), PM (promethazine), antihistamines

Preferred Antidepressants (Geriatric)

  • Sertraline: Best evidence in elderly depression; start 25mg → 50–100mg
  • Escitalopram: Max 10mg in elderly (QTc at higher doses); cleanest CYP
  • Mirtazapine: Appetite stimulation, sleep, weight gain benefit; start 7.5mg
  • Bupropion: Avoid if seizure risk; useful for fatigue/apathy; lowers seizure threshold
  • Venlafaxine: BP monitoring; useful if pain comorbidity
  • Avoid: Paroxetine (anticholinergic), TCAs, MAOIs as first-line

Dementia & Behavioral Symptoms

  • Cholinesterase Inhibitors: Donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine — mild-moderate AD; GI side effects
  • Memantine: Moderate-severe AD; NMDA antagonist; additive with ChEI
  • Lecanemab (Leqembi): Anti-amyloid mAb; early AD; ARIA monitoring required; REMS
  • BPSD (Behavioral/Psych Symptoms): Try non-pharm first; if meds needed: low-dose risperidone or quetiapine — black box warning re: mortality but acceptable risk in severe cases
  • Agitation in dementia: Brexpiprazole (Rexulti) — only FDA-approved for agitation in AD (2023)

Dosing Adjustments

  • Renal: Lithium, gabapentin, pregabalin — dose by CrCl; hold if GFR <30
  • Hepatic: Lamotrigine, valproate — reduce dose; monitor levels
  • Half-life increases: diazepam, clonazepam accumulate → use lorazepam (no active metabolites)
  • Protein binding decreases → higher free drug levels → enhanced effects/toxicity
  • Always check: Falls risk, cognitive burden, QTc, orthostatic BP

Clinical Pearls

  • Late-onset psychosis: rule out Lewy body, vascular dementia, UTI, metabolic
  • Depression in elderly often presents with somatic complaints and cognitive slowing (pseudodementia)
  • Assess polypharmacy at EVERY visit — medication burden is underrecognized
  • Lithium can be used in elderly but requires more frequent monitoring; goal level 0.4–0.6 mEq/L
  • Avoid sedating agents in anyone with fall history; assess orthostatic BP

Substance Use Disorders

ASAM framework | FDA-approved pharmacotherapy | Harm reduction | Comorbidity is the rule, not exception

Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD)

  • Naltrexone (PO/IM): First-line; blocks opioid reward; 50mg/day or 380mg IM monthly; avoid with opioid use
  • Acamprosate: First-line; glutamate modulation; no hepatic metabolism; 666mg TID; use if opioid user
  • Disulfiram: Aversion therapy; requires abstinence before starting; high adherence needed; hepatotoxicity risk
  • Gabapentin: Emerging evidence; 900–1800mg/day; improves sleep and craving; not FDA-approved
  • Topiramate: Off-label; craving reduction; cognitive side effects limit use
  • Withdrawal management: CIWA protocol; lorazepam (oxazepam in liver disease); phenobarbital for severe cases

Opioid Use Disorder (OUD)

  • Buprenorphine/Naloxone (Suboxone): Gold standard outpatient; partial mu agonist; ceiling effect for respiratory depression; START at 8/2mg → titrate to comfort
  • Methadone: Full mu agonist; OTP only (federally regulated clinics); highest efficacy; QTc monitoring required
  • Naltrexone XR (Vivitrol): Monthly IM; full abstinence required; ideal for high-motivation patients; no abuse potential
  • Naloxone (Narcan): Prescribe to all OUD patients AND household members
  • COWS scale for withdrawal assessment; do NOT start buprenorphine until COWS ≥8–12

Tobacco Use Disorder

  • Varenicline (Chantix): Most effective (2× vs NRT); partial nicotinic agonist; GI side effects; monitor mood
  • Bupropion SR: Second-line; dual purpose if depression comorbid; 150mg BID x 12 weeks
  • NRT (patch, gum, lozenge): OTC; safest; combine forms for higher success rate
  • Counseling + pharmacotherapy outperforms either alone

Cannabis & Stimulant Use

  • Cannabis: No FDA-approved pharmacotherapy; worsens psychosis risk; N-acetylcysteine studied in adolescents; behavioral therapy primary
  • Stimulant (cocaine/meth): No FDA-approved pharmacotherapy; contingency management most effective behavioral intervention; bupropion studied; topiramate, modafinil emerging
  • For stimulant psychosis: short-term low-dose antipsychotic; clarify duration to distinguish from primary psychosis

Clinical Pearls

  • ASAM CRITERIA: assess all 6 dimensions before placing level of care
  • Treat comorbid psychiatric illness — poorly treated MDD/PTSD drives relapse
  • MOUD (Medication for OUD) reduces mortality by 50%; document counseling on benefits
  • Stimulants are NOT contraindicated in ADHD + SUD (in remission or stable) — treat both
  • Use motivational interviewing — ambivalence is normal; roll with resistance

TRD & Complex Cases

Treatment-Resistant Depression | Staging systems | Augmentation hierarchy | Novel interventions

TRD Definition & Staging

  • Standard: Failure of ≥2 adequate trials (therapeutic dose × ≥6 weeks, confirmed adherence)
  • Thase-Rush Staging: Stage I (1 failed trial) → Stage V (ECT failure)
  • MARDS: Factors include number of trials, dose adequacy, duration, ECT history
  • Always re-evaluate diagnosis before labeling TRD: bipolar? ADHD? personality? medical? substance use?
  • Consider: Pharmacogenomic testing (GeneSight, Genomind) before next trial

Augmentation Strategies

  • Atypical AP + SSRI/SNRI: Aripiprazole (7.5–15mg), Quetiapine XR (50–300mg), Brexpiprazole (2–3mg) — all FDA-approved MDD adjunct
  • Lithium augmentation: Goal level 0.6–1.0 mEq/L; proven strategy; use when others fail
  • Mirtazapine + SSRI/SNRI (California Rocket Fuel): Robust augmentation; weight + sedation trade-offs
  • Thyroid (T3): Liothyronine 25–50mcg; may enhance antidepressant response even in euthyroid patients
  • Buspirone: 15–60mg/day; partial 5-HT1A agonist; well-tolerated; modest evidence
  • Lamotrigine: Augment SSRI/SNRI; especially useful bipolar depression component

Novel & Rapid-Acting Treatments

  • Esketamine (Spravato): FDA-approved TRD + acute suicidality; NMDA antagonist; intranasal; REMS; 2× weekly × 4 weeks induction then weekly/biweekly; observe 2h post-dose
  • IV Ketamine: Off-label; 0.5mg/kg over 40 min; rapid onset hours; 6 infusion series; not FDA-approved; clinic-based
  • ECT: Most effective treatment for severe TRD; faster than meds; indicated in psychotic depression, catatonia, imminent suicidality
  • TMS (rTMS/dTMS): FDA-cleared; noninvasive; 36 sessions (standard) or 5-day iTBS; moderate evidence; no systemic SE
  • Psilocybin: Phase III trials; breakthrough therapy designation; not yet FDA-approved; transformative in TRD/end-of-life depression

Clinical Pearls

  • Confirm adherence before declaring treatment failure — plasma levels, pill counts, pharmacy refill history
  • Adequate trial = correct dose × ≥6 weeks; many providers under-dose or undertrial
  • Esketamine must be administered in certified healthcare setting; document monitoring
  • ECT referral should not be a "last resort" — advocate early for severe/psychotic depression
  • Genetic testing helps but is not diagnostic — use as one tool; CYP2D6/2C19 most clinically relevant
  • Consider neuroimmune workup in truly refractory cases: inflammatory markers, autoimmune encephalitis panel

Pharmacogenomic Testing

Pharmacogenomics identifies how genetic variants affect drug metabolism and response. Most clinically relevant enzymes in psychiatry:

  • CYP2D6: Metabolizes SSRIs, TCAs, antipsychotics, atomoxetine, codeine. PM → toxicity; UM → treatment failure
  • CYP2C19: Metabolizes SSRIs (citalopram, escitalopram, sertraline), diazepam. PM → higher drug levels
  • CYP3A4: Metabolizes many antipsychotics, benzodiazepines. Inducible by carbamazepine, smoking
  • MTHFR: Folate/methylation; C677T and A1298C variants → L-methylfolate supplementation
  • SLC6A4 (5-HTTLPR): Serotonin transporter; s/s allele → lower SSRI response, higher stress reactivity
  • COMT Val158Met: Catechol-O-methyltransferase; Met/Met → better working memory; Val/Val → more dopamine breakdown

Commercial panels: GeneSight, Genomind, RxSight, Genomics Simplified. Insurance coverage varies — document clinical justification.

Exercise as Medicine

  • Depression: 150 min/week moderate aerobic = 50mg sertraline (SMILE trial). Effect mediated by BDNF, serotonin, NE release
  • Anxiety: Both aerobic and resistance training reduce anxiety; aerobic stronger for acute relief
  • ADHD: Single bout of aerobic exercise improves executive function for 30–60 min afterward (use before homework)
  • PTSD/Trauma: Bilateral stimulation (walking, running) aids trauma processing; somatic movement (yoga, dance) releases stored tension
  • Practical: Start with 10-min walks after meals; use habit stacking; pair with existing routines

Nutrition & Gut-Brain Axis

  • Mediterranean Diet: ~30% lower depression risk; anti-inflammatory; rich in omega-3, polyphenols, fiber
  • Gut microbiome: ~90% of serotonin produced in gut; dysbiosis → mood, anxiety, cognitive symptoms
  • High-yield foods: Fatty fish (EPA/DHA), leafy greens (folate), fermented foods (lactobacillus → GABA), berries (anthocyanins), walnuts, dark chocolate
  • Eliminate: Ultra-processed foods, refined sugars (glucose spikes → mood instability), artificial dyes (ADHD), trans fats (inflammation)
  • GLP-1 agonists: Weight management if metabolic syndrome; psychiatric implications being studied

Nervous System Regulation

  • Polyvagal-Informed: Safety is the treatment; co-regulation before self-regulation; ventral vagal → calm/social; dorsal vagal → shutdown
  • Vagal Tone Techniques: Humming, singing, gargling, cold water face immersion, slow exhalation (4-7-8 breathing), laughter
  • HRV Biofeedback: 5.5 breaths/minute resonance frequency; apps: HeartMath, Elite HRV
  • Somatic practices: Body scanning, progressive muscle relaxation, trauma-sensitive yoga, EMDR-adjacent movement
  • Cold exposure: Cold shower 30–60sec → norepinephrine +300%; antidepressant effect; start gradually
  • Heat therapy (sauna): 20 min sauna → endorphin + BDNF release; cardiovascular benefit; not appropriate for all patients

Sleep Optimization

  • CBT-I: Most effective treatment for chronic insomnia — outperforms all medications long-term. Components: Sleep restriction, stimulus control, cognitive restructuring
  • Circadian entrainment: Morning sunlight within 30 min of waking (no sunglasses); anchors cortisol awakening response
  • Sleep hygiene non-negotiables: Consistent wake time (even weekends), cool room (65–68°F), darkness, no caffeine after 12–2 PM
  • Screens: Blue light blocking after 9 PM; night mode; or 2h screen-free before bed
  • Psychiatric context: Sleep disruption worsens ALL psychiatric conditions; treat sleep proactively, not as afterthought

Stress Regulation

  • Allostatic load: Cumulative burden of stress on biological systems; assess via HRV, cortisol rhythm, inflammatory markers
  • Mindfulness (MBSR): 8-week protocol; reduces amygdala reactivity; cortical thickening in prefrontal cortex; validated in anxiety, depression, chronic pain
  • Breathwork: Cyclic sighing (2:1 exhale:inhale ratio) most effective for real-time stress relief; box breathing (4-4-4-4) for focus
  • Nature exposure: 20 min in nature → cortisol ↓ 21%; "green prescriptions" gaining evidence base
  • Social connection: Isolation → cortisol ↑, immune dysregulation; prioritize as medical intervention

Light & Chronobiology

  • Light therapy: 10,000 lux lamp; 20–30 min upon waking; most evidence in seasonal depression; emerging for bipolar depression, non-seasonal MDD
  • SAD: Light therapy = antidepressant in seasonal MDD; can combine with bupropion for augmentation
  • Bipolar caveat: Light therapy can trigger mania in bipolar I — use cautiously, add mood stabilizer cover
  • Blue light AM: Critical for suppressing melatonin and anchoring circadian rhythm; daylight walk preferred over lamp when possible
  • Melatonin timing: 0.5–1mg taken 2–3h before desired bedtime is more effective than high-dose at bedtime

Neurotransmitter Systems

    
Receptor pharmacology, symptom clusters, and drug targets

    
For licensed healthcare providers only. Educational use only. Not medical advice.

  


  


    

      

        
5-HT Serotonin

      

      

        
Key Receptors5-HT1A (autoreceptor/anxiolytic), 5-HT2A (antidepressant/psychedelic), 5-HT2C (appetite/weight), 5-HT3 (nausea — blocking is therapeutic), 5-HT4 (GI motility)

        
Low Serotonin SxDepression, anxiety, OCD, impulsivity, aggression, carbohydrate craving, sleep disruption

        
High Serotonin (excess)Serotonin syndrome: tremor, diaphoresis, hyperthermia, clonus, agitation — medical emergency

        
Drug MechanismsSSRIs/SNRIs: block SERT reuptake → ↑ synaptic 5-HT. Buspirone: 5-HT1A partial agonist. Trazodone/nefazodone: 5-HT2A antagonist + SERT block. MAOIs: prevent breakdown. Atypical APs: 5-HT2A antagonism (augments DA in PFC)

        
Clinical Pearl5-HT2A antagonism (atypicals) can paradoxically improve depression + cognition by disinhibiting dopamine in prefrontal cortex. This is why SGAs work as antidepressant augmenters.

      

    


    

      

        
DA Dopamine

      

      

        
Key PathwaysMesolimbic (reward/motivation/pleasure), Mesocortical (cognition/executive function), Nigrostriatal (motor control), Tuberoinfundibular (prolactin regulation)

        
Key ReceptorsD1 (cognition, PFC — stimulants target), D2 (reward, motor, nausea — antipsychotics block), D3 (motivation), D4 (cognition/mood)

        
Low DA SxAnhedonia, amotivation, fatigue, cognitive slowing, ADHD-like symptoms, low libido, apathy

        
High DA SxPsychosis (positive symptoms), mania, hypersexuality, addiction, impulsivity

        
Drug MechanismsStimulants: block DAT (reuptake) → ↑ DA. Antipsychotics: D2 antagonists (typicals strong, atypicals partial/lower affinity). Aripiprazole: D2 partial agonist. Bupropion: DAT/NET inhibitor. Cariprazine: D3/D2 partial agonist

        
Clinical PearlAntipsychotic-induced hyperprolactinemia = tuberoinfundibular DA blockade. Risperidone/paliperidone are highest risk; aripiprazole/quetiapine are lowest. Check prolactin if sexual dysfunction, amenorrhea, or galactorrhea.

      

    


    

      

        
NE Norepinephrine

      

      

        
Key ReceptorsAlpha-1 (vasoconstriction, attention), Alpha-2 (autoreceptor — inhibits NE release; target of clonidine/guanfacine), Beta-1 (cardiac output), Beta-2 (bronchodilation)

        
Low NE SxFatigue, poor concentration, low energy, cognitive slowing, depression (especially melancholic), ADHD (inattentive subtype), orthostatic hypotension

        
High NE SxAnxiety, panic attacks, hypertension, tachycardia, tremor, hypervigilance, PTSD hyperarousal

        
Drug MechanismsSNRIs: block SERT + NET → ↑ NE + 5-HT. Atomoxetine: selective NET inhibitor (non-stimulant ADHD). Mirtazapine: alpha-2 antagonist → disinhibits NE release. TCAs: NET inhibitors (secondary amines > tertiary). Prazosin: alpha-1 blocker → reduces PTSD nightmares

        
Clinical PearlPrazosin's benefit in PTSD nightmares is from alpha-1 blockade attenuating the noradrenergic storm during REM sleep. Start 1mg at bedtime, titrate slowly to 3–15mg. Monitor for orthostatic hypotension.

      

    


    

      

        
GABA GABA

      

      

        
Key ReceptorsGABA-A (ionotropic Cl⁻ channel — fast inhibition; benzodiazepine/barbiturate/alcohol site), GABA-B (metabotropic — baclofen/GHB)

        
Low GABA SxAnxiety, panic, insomnia, seizures, muscle tension, hyperexcitability, alcohol withdrawal

        
High GABA SxSedation, respiratory depression, cognitive impairment, memory impairment, disinhibition (paradoxical in elderly)

        
Drug MechanismsBenzodiazepines: positive allosteric modulators at GABA-A → ↑ Cl⁻ influx frequency. Z-drugs: similar to BZDs but more selective for omega-1 subunit. Pregabalin/gabapentin: alpha-2-delta subunit binders → ↓ Ca2+ influx → ↓ glutamate/NE release. Brexanolone/zuranolone: neurosteroid PAMs at synaptic + extrasynaptic GABA-A

        
Clinical PearlBenzodiazepines increase the FREQUENCY of Cl⁻ channel opening. Barbiturates increase the DURATION. This is why barbiturates are more dangerous in overdose — no ceiling on GABA potentiation.

      

    


    

      

        
Glu Glutamate

      

      

        
Key ReceptorsNMDA (learning/memory/LTP; Mg2+ voltage-gated; ketamine target), AMPA (fast excitation; potentiated by ketamine downstream), mGluR (modulatory)

        
Excess Glutamate SxNeurotoxicity, excitotoxicity (stroke/TBI), psychosis (NMDA hypofunction → DA excess), cognitive decline, mania

        
Drug MechanismsKetamine/esketamine: NMDA antagonist → rapid AMPA up-regulation → BDNF release → synaptic growth (within hours). Memantine: NMDA antagonist (moderate affinity) → neuroprotective in Alzheimer. Lamotrigine: blocks glutamate release via Na channel. Acamprosate: modulates NMDA/GABA balance in AUD

        
Clinical PearlKetamine's rapid antidepressant effect (hours vs weeks) is from AMPA receptor upregulation and BDNF surge — NOT just NMDA blockade. This reverses stress-induced synaptic loss in PFC/hippocampus. It's not just sedation or dissociation.

      

    


    

      

        
ACh Acetylcholine

      

      

        
Key ReceptorsNicotinic (ionotropic — fast; cognition, reward, muscle contraction), Muscarinic M1-M5 (M1: cognition; M2: cardiac; M3: GI/glands; M4: striatum; M5: reward)

        
Low ACh SxCognitive impairment, memory loss, constipation, urinary retention, dry mouth, blurred vision, delirium (anticholinergic toxidrome)

        
High ACh SxBradycardia, hypersalivation, diarrhea, sweating, miosis, bronchospasm (cholinergic crisis — organophosphate toxicity)

        
Drug MechanismsChEIs (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine): block AChE → ↑ ACh in synaptic cleft → Alzheimer treatment. Anticholinergics (benztropine, diphenhydramine): M-receptor antagonists → EPS treatment but cognitive risk. Clozapine: strong M-antagonist → salivation paradox (M4 agonism via secondary metabolite)

        
Clinical PearlAnticholinergic burden is additive. Score medications: diphenhydramine, amitriptyline, paroxetine, benztropine, oxybutynin all contribute. High cumulative burden = delirium risk, cognitive impairment, falls in elderly. Use the Anticholinergic Cognitive Burden (ACB) scale.
